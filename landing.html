Executive summary HADR Monitor — at a glance

In the first hours of a disaster the bottleneck is never effort — it's information: scattered across feeds, arriving faster than anyone can read, and thick with duplicates and false alarms. HADR Monitor is an unattended agent that reconciles USGS, GDACS and ReliefWeb into one decision-ready picture and briefs your team every morning at 08:30 — leading with what changed since yesterday, including anything it previously reported that is no longer true.

Provisional → confirmed

Never mistake an early read for a settled one.

The first minutes of a quake are the fastest information you'll get — and the least certain. Magnitude and location land before any impact signal does. HADR Monitor labels every event provisional on first detection and firms it to confirmed the moment the data settles — a reviewed USGS status, a PAGER alert, or a GDACS ShakeMap. Your team sees at a glance exactly how much to trust each line.

  • Status on every event. An early, automatic read is never dressed up as settled fact.
  • Headline vs. below the fold. Material events lead; routine and ongoing ones fold into a tidy summary so the top of the brief stays clean.
  • Sticky. Once an event is confirmed it never quietly slips back to provisional.

The decision it speeds up

Where to send scarce responders, supplies, and attention — and how fast. Instead of a morning scramble across a dozen feeds, coordinators open one brief, see what changed overnight, and commit resources on facts. The minutes saved at the start of a response are the ones that save the most lives.

The feature no one else has

It tells you what changed — including what it got wrong.

Disaster feeds are living databases: magnitudes get revised, locations shift, events are retracted. Almost every other tool reports once and never looks back. HADR Monitor's briefing leads with what changed since the last brief — upgrades, downgrades, and honest retractions of anything it previously told you.

  • Self-correcting. Yesterday's picture is reconciled against today's data, so your team never acts on stale numbers.
  • Builds trust. Corrections are phrased plainly, not buried — the report earns confidence by owning its revisions.
  • Change-first. You read the delta, not the whole world, every morning — seconds to orient, not minutes.

How it works

Four moving parts. Zero babysitting.

A fast loop keeps a reconciled picture current; the morning tick turns it into prose your responders can read in a minute.

01 · WATCH

Poll the feeds

Every ~30 minutes it fetches GDACS, USGS and ReliefWeb — efficiently, within each source's rate budget.

02 · RECONCILE

Merge into one truth

The deterministic core matches events across feeds and keeps a single canonical record per real-world event.

03 · ASSESS

Judge severity

Impact is scored; severe, high-confidence events trigger an immediate push instead of waiting.

04 · BRIEF

Publish at 08:30

It diffs against yesterday and writes a self-correcting report to dashboard.html — then goes quiet.

Clear boundaries

What it deliberately does not do.

Good situational awareness knows its own limits. Being explicit about what HADR Monitor is not is what keeps its briefings trustworthy — and tells your team exactly where to look elsewhere.

Replace official warnings

It complements authoritative alerting, it doesn't supersede it. A feed flag is not a warning — a tsunami marker here is not a NOAA tsunami warning, for instance.

Cover every hazard — yet

Earthquakes are handled end to end today. Landslides, extreme heat and conflict are out of scope, and flood coverage is late and patchy. Every brief names these blind spots.

Republish others' reporting

Humanitarian report bodies stay with their authors. You get a link, a short excerpt and attribution — never a wholesale copy.

Cry wolf

It stays silent when nothing has changed and interrupts only for severe, high-confidence events — so an alert always means something.

Decide for you

Automated reconciliation and assessment inform the call; responders make it. Human judgement stays firmly in the loop.

Guess silently

When signals are provisional or missing, it says so — labelling a first read as provisional until impact data confirms it.
